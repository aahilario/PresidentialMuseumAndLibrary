PRESIDENT QUEZON SPEAKS. 




FOR THE PEOPLE: A TOAST FROM THE PRESIDENT ON SOCIAL JUSTICE. 




I 1 





EARLY INDEPENDENCE and Social Justice have lately-been 
President Quezon's favorite political songhits. In a speech 
remarkable for its sane statesmanship the President once 
again Monday afternoon, Oct. 18, before the Assembly en- 
larged on these two crackerjacks, drove home his points with 
telling effect. Attended by bigwigs was the gathering which 
was supposed to have opened the regular session of the legis- 
lature. 

Emphasized President Quezon: "An opportunity has 
been given us that no past or coming generation has had or 
will ever have — that of creating a nation where there will be 
no privileged class, where poverty will be unknown. ..." 
On the traditional cry for independence he pontificated : "My 
own firm conviction is that it is better for the Filipino people 
to be independent now . . . rather than in 1946." He said he 
saw no reason why, "if the Philippines can be given independ- 
ence in 1946, she may not be granted it in 1938 or 1939." 

Given the nut to crack, several assemblymen a few days 
after Quezon's speech drafted multifarious resolutions favoring 
early freedom for the Philippines. Also subject to speculation 
was possibility of creating an upper legislative chamber, mem- 
bers of which are to be elected at large.
